Among the vast field of Democrats vying for the 2020 presidential nomination there is a broad acceptance that the climate crisis must be confronted. However, there is a split between those who have signed a pledge to no longer accept money from the oil, gas and coal industry and those who haven't
Of those who haven't signed the pledge, frontrunner Joe Biden, New York mayor Bill de Blasio and senator Kamala Harris have said they won't take fossil fuel money. This week, Biden released a climate plan that promised action "well beyond" what the Obama administration achieved.
Prior to signing the pledge, nearly all of the 2020 Democratic candidates took money from fossil fuel executives, other employees and via political action committees (PACs)

Source: OpenSecrets, includes all federal donations from 1989 to May 2019

New York senator Kristen Gillibrand, who has signed the pledge, has taken around half of her fossil fuel contributions via PACs. Conversely, Bernie Sanders hasn't taken any dollars via PACs but has taken money from people who work in the oil and gas sector
As the politics over global heating has become polarized, so have industry donations. Once split fairly evenly, fossil fuel companies are now overwhelmingly funding Republicans rather than Democrats. In the 2016 election period alone, Donald Trump received nearly $1.3m from the sector

Methodology

Pledge signees were taken from No Fossil Fuel Money Pledge at the time of publication. Political donation data was provided by OpenSecrets was at a federal level only. This means that some candidates, including those who have $0 next to their name, potentially have taken donations while running for state or city offices earlier in their career.
